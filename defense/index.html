Reading the threat (the Taiwanese-specific skill) ›
With no riichi call, you infer danger from what's visible:

Called melds

Each open meld means three fewer hidden tiles and a hand closer to completion. A called value pung — dragons, or the round/seat wind — signals a hand that's both advanced and expensive. Defend hardest against these.

Tempo & discards

Fast, confident discards of useless tiles suggest a settled, near-ready hand. Late discards of middle tiles can mean a hand reshaping at the last moment.

Your own position

Defense is a trade-off. If you're far from your own win, folding is cheap. If you're also ready with a strong hand, pushing is often right — you don't fold a good hand out of fear. This is judgement, not a formula.
Safe-tile principles & their limits ›

現物 — their own discards

A tile in an opponent's river can't be won on if furiten / 過水 is enforced (common, not universal — confirm your table). The single most reliable defensive tile when the rule applies.

Honors & terminals

An honor can only be a pair or twin-pung wait; a terminal only an edge or closed wait. Fewer shapes reach them, so they're lower risk — never zero.

The wall (壁 / no-chance)

If all four copies of a connecting tile are visible, certain two-sided waits become impossible, making a tile safer. This is pure counting and doesn't depend on furiten — but it only rules out sequence waits.

筋 (suji)

If they discarded a 4, then 1 and 7 are a little safer against a two-sided wait — but only that wait, only under furiten, and weaker here than in riichi since you can't be sure they're ready. Treat it as a tiebreaker, never a guarantee.
